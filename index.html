The Second Half of Unified Models

Paper Code Data

GENIUS is the first benchmark designed to purely quantify Generative Fluid Intelligence (GFI). It features 510 expert-curated samples spanning three dimensions and five tasks.

Unified Multimodal Models (UMMs) have shown remarkable progress in visual generation. Yet, existing benchmarks predominantly assess Crystallized Intelligence, which relies on recalling accumulated knowledge and learned schemas. This focus overlooks Generative Fluid Intelligence (GFI): the capacity to induce patterns, reason through constraints, and adapt to novel scenarios on the fly. To rigorously assess this capability, we introduce GENIUS (GENerative Fluid Intelligence EvalUation Suite). We formalize GFI as a synthesis of three primitives: (1) Inducing Implicit Patterns (e.g., inferring personalized visual preferences), (2) Executing Ad-hoc Constraints (e.g., visualizing abstract metaphors), and (3) Adapting to Contextual Knowledge (e.g., simulating counter-intuitive physics). Our systematic evaluation of 12 representative models reveals significant performance deficits in these tasks. Crucially, our diagnostic analysis disentangles these failure modes, demonstrating that deficits stem from limited context comprehension rather than insufficient intrinsic generative capability. To bridge this gap, we propose a training-free attention intervention strategy. Ultimately, GENIUS establishes a rigorous standard for GFI, guiding the field beyond knowledge utilization toward dynamic, general-purpose reasoning.

Dataset Statistics

GENIUS comprises a total of 510 expert-curated samples spanning 20 diverse sub-tasks. The dataset is structurally distributed across three core dimensions: Implicit Pattern Induction (86 samples), Ad-hoc Constraint Execution (213 samples), and Contextual Knowledge Adaptation (211 samples).

Diagnostic Analysis

1. The Execution Gap & Metric Validation

Why do models fail? We identify a significant "Execution Gap": models accurately comprehend context (high VQA accuracy) yet fail to execute it in generation.
Is the judge reliable? Our validation shows the LMM-as-a-Judge metric achieves a 0.96+ Pearson correlation with human experts, ensuring evaluation reliability.

2. Visualizing the Cause: Noisy Gradients

Further attention analysis reveals the root cause: existing models suffer from Noisy Gradients due to distracted attention on irrelevant tokens. Our method successfully suppresses noise and focuses attention on critical signal tokens, resulting in a Clean Gradient.

Methodology

To address the "Noisy Gradient" issue identified above, we propose a training-free attention intervention strategy. By viewing In-Context Learning (ICL) as implicit fine-tuning, we employ a three-stage pipeline: Keyword Distillation, Relevance Mapping, and Bias Injection to rectify the optimization direction.
